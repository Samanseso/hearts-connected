CHAPTER I:
1.1 Background of the study
Media has become an integral part of the daily lives of adolescents, and this
is evident in the way they view themselves, others, and love as well. Smith (2025),
investigates the effect of social media and romantic fiction on the understanding of love
of high school students. With media platforms and fiction becoming a constant in the
lives of teenagers, it is necessary to know how these sources influence their relationship
ideals. At Our Lady of Guadalupe Colleges (OLGC), the Senior High School and College
students are always exposed to different media types, which include television dramas,
online series, social networking sites, or short-form video platforms. These media are
constantly with them and are considered the main source of information, possibly
influencing the way they perceive commitment, dating norms, and romantic expectations.
Research gap
Many studies have already looked at how social media affects young people’s
ideas about love and relationships, but most of these were done in other countries and not
focused on Filipino students (Bouffard et al.,2022). Also, most research only shows
connections and not the actual cause-and-effect of media on relationship views (Culfa et
al., 2024). Other than that, most of them focus on adults who are already in relationships

2.1.1 Cultivation Theory
According to Cultivation Theory, seeing romantic stories and posts over and
over can slowly change how students think about dating and love. Students’ attitudes
toward dating are about how they see dating, whether it is just for fun or something
serious.
2.1.2 Social Learning Theory
Social Learning Theory says students can copy what they see in media, like
how people act in shows or posts. Students’ attitudes toward romantic commitment are
about how much they value staying loyal, being serious in a relationship, or wanting
long-term love. Watching the media all the time can influence whether students think
commitment is important or not.
In short, media can affect both how students think about dating and how much
they care about commitment. This framework helps explain the connections this study
wants to look at.

2.2 Conceptual Framework
The independent variable in this study is Media Exposure, which includes
students’ engagement with social media platforms, romantic-themed content, and dating
applications. This exposure is hypothesized to influence the dependent variable:
Students’ Attitudes Toward Dating and Romantic Commitment.
Students’ attitudes refer to their beliefs, expectations, and emotional responses
related to romantic relationships. These attitudes may be shaped by the frequency, type,
and nature of media consumed. As these attitudes evolve, they are expected to affect the
level of Romantic Commitment, which includes emotional investment, exclusivity, and
long-term intentions.
This framework supports the formulation of hypotheses and guides the
interpretation of statistical findings. It also aligns with the theoretical foundations of
Cultivation Theory and Sexual Script Theory, which explain how media shapes
perceptions, expectations, and behavioral norms in romantic contexts.

2.1.1.2 Social Media and Relationship Behavior
Beyond shaping ideals, media exposure particularly through social networking
sites has been found to influence individuals’ relational behaviors and levels of
commitment. Abbasi (2021) reported that Facebook addiction was positively linked to
romantic disengagement and infidelity among younger users, while Li (2023) revealed
that social media use can foster dissatisfaction, weaken emotional connections, and
reduce trust in intimate relationships. Similarly, Lewyckyj and Glavin (2023) observed
that permissive sexual attitudes are associated with greater dating app use and lower
commitment levels. Supporting this, Rittenburgh-Kirby (2020) concluded that the
perception of abundant partner options on dating platforms diminishes romantic
commitment by encouraging indecision and emotional detachment. Taken together, these
studies indicate that media exposure not only shapes individuals’ beliefs but also
significantly alters their relational behaviors and commitment patterns, particularly
among students.
In recent years, researchers have explored how social media platforms—particularly
Instagram—affect the emotional dynamics and stability of romantic relationships.
Bouffard, Giglio, and Zheng (2021) explored the complex effects of excessive
Instagram use on romantic relationships, revealing a mediated pathway where increased
usage leads to reduced relationship satisfaction, which in turn contributes to heightened
conflict and social media addiction. Their structural equation modeling approach

2.1.1.3 Student Attitudes and Perceptions
Students’ personal experiences and reflections play a crucial role in shaping
their romantic attitudes. Jamison and Sanner (2021) examined how young adults interpret
their romantic and sexual histories, highlighting the development of emotional maturity,
boundary-setting, and partner preferences through reflection. Similarly, an anonymous
study found that exposure to glamorous social media portrayals often triggers jealousy
and dissatisfaction, thereby weakening commitment in relationships. Tan (2022) explored
perfectionism among Filipino students and revealed that both romantic ideals and
academic pressure are heavily influenced by rigid family expectations and media
representations. In support of this, Bartolome, et. al., (2020) observed that authoritarian
parenting styles, which emphasize discipline and obedience, tend to foster emotional
suppression and internal conflict, consequently shaping students’ romantic perceptions
and emotional resilience.

2.3. International Studies
Many students today form their ideas about love and commitment not
from personal experience, but from what they constantly see on social media. Romantic
posts, idealized relationships, and dramatic portrayals often shape how they think
relationships should look and feel. Hatamleh (2024) emphasized that repeated exposure
to romantic content can lead students to expect perfection in their own relationships.
Horan (2023) added that constant online communication may distort how people view
commitment, creating expectations that don’t always match reality.
Social media also influences how students compare themselves to others.
Kach and Segal (2021) explained that online platforms blend personal values with
unrealistic standards, which can lead to insecurity and self-doubt. Similarly, Çulfa,
Güven, and Doğan (2024) found that media portrayals affect how students define
happiness and success in romantic relationships. These idealized images often become
the basis for how young people measure their own experiences, even if those portrayals
are far from realistic.

2.5 Synthesis of Literature
Across recent studies, the media is known to be a significant factor in shaping
students' emotional well being, romantic ideals, and commitment behaviours. It is
consistently noted by researchers that the media promotes idealised portrayals of love and
romantic relationships, which can lead to emotional fatigue and unrealistic expectations
in real relationships (Hatamleh, 2024; Horan, 2023; Kach & Segal, 2021). Moreover,
these idealizations interact with cultural norms and familial expectations, which intensify
the emotional strain among students who struggle, or are unable to meet such standards
(Tan, 2022; Bartolome et al., 2020).
Beyond attitudes, several studies highlight the behavioral implications of media
exposure, such as disengagement, infidelity, and decreased relational satisfaction
(Abbasi, 2021; Li, 2023; Rittenburgh-Kirby, 2020). These outcomes are often increased
by authoritarian parenting styles and the pressure to obey socially constructed images of
romance (Tan, 2022; Perez et al., 2022).

3.1 Research design
This study uses a correlational design, a type of quantitative research that aims
to identify whether a relationship exists between two variables and to determine the
strength and direction of that relationship.
3.2 Context and Participants
The study will be conducted at the Our Lady of Guadalupe Colleges campus
during the early part of the second semester, specifically between January and February
2025. This location was chosen due to its accessibility and the researchers’ familiarity
with its academic environment, which supports efficient and timely data collection.
Participants will consist of senior high school and college students aged 18 to
21 years old who are currently enrolled in the institution. A total of 50 students will be
selected through purposive sampling, ensuring that respondents meet the criteria relevant
to the study’s objectives.
The selected age range is significant as it represents a developmental stage in
late adolescence where individuals begin to form independent views and relationship
attitudes. According to the World Health Organization (2022), adolescence spans from

● Part III: Romantic Commitment – emotional investment, exclusivity, and

3.4 Data Gathering Procedure
The researchers will develop a structured survey questionnaire composed of
close-ended items. The instrument will be adapted from the Emotional Maturity Scale
(EMS) originally developed by Singh and Bhargava (1990), and referenced in the 2023
local study by Malijao et al., titled “Emotional Maturity and Level of Self-Reliance of the
Children of Overseas Filipino Workers.” This study validated the EMS among Filipino
adolescents, confirming its reliability and cultural relevance.
3.4.1 Procedure for Research Instrument
The primary instrument used in this study is a questionnaire, consisting of a
checklist and a likert scale. The researchers will explain the components to the
respondents. The actual data gathering will take place within the school premises and the
respondents will be given enough time to answer the questions. The questionnaire is
divided into three parts:
● Part I: The researchers gather the demographic profile of the respondents,
including age, sex, and the department where they belonged (Nursing,
Medical Technology, Radiologic Technology, Senior High School).
● Part II: The researchers will use a questionnaire to identify the respondents
attitude and views towards romantic commitment.

3.5 Statistical Treatment of Data
The data gathered from the questionnaire was coded and analyzed using
descriptive and inferential statistics. The demographic profile of the respondents was
summarised by using frequencies and summaries, while means and standard deviations
was computed to describe their levels of social media exposure and their attitudes
towards romantic commitment. The reliability of the Likert-scale items was tested using
Chronbach’s alpha. To determine the relationship between social media exposure and
students' attitude towards romantic commitment, the researchers used either Pearson's r or
Spearman's rho, depending on the data’s normality. All statistical tests were interpreted at
the significance level of 0.05.
